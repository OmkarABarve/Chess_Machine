## 🔍 Deep Dive: V1 vs. V2 vs. V3

### 🧱 Version 1: The Flat Feed-Forward Network

* **What it is:** You flatten the 64 squares of the chess board into 64 continuous input numbers (0 for empty, 1 for White Pawn, etc.) and feed them straight into standard dense layers (`nn.Linear`).
* **Chess Intelligence & Behavior:** It treats the board like a fixed grid. It will learn basic spatial configurations (e.g., "If there is a 1 on square 12 and a 12 on square 20, move X is often good"). However, it treats a White Knight (`2`) and a Black King (`12`) purely as arbitrary numbers. It doesn't inherently know that a Knight moves differently or that two different pieces are both "Knights".
* **Primary Use:** A quick, working baseline. It proves your data pipeline (PGN parsing, Parquet saving, training loop) actually works without complex bugs.
* **Why it's good for Goal A:** It is the fastest route to a move-generator that can play a cohesive game of chess at a beginner level.

### 💎 Version 2: The Embedding Upgrade

* **What it is:** Instead of passing the raw numbers `0` to `12` directly into the network, you insert the `nn.Embedding` layer we discussed earlier. Every piece type gets converted into a dense vector (e.g., a vector of 16 or 32 numbers) that changes and updates during training.
* **Chess Intelligence & Behavior:** The model develops a primitive "understanding" of piece identities. During training, the vector for a White Knight and a Black Knight will naturally be driven to share similar geometric traits because they share similar movement capabilities, while an empty square vector will look entirely different.
* **Primary Use:** Cleaner generalization. If the model sees a board state it has never encountered before, its understanding of "what a piece is" allows it to make a much smarter guess than Version 1.
* **Novelty:** Introduces representation learning. You can pull the weights out of the embedding layer, plot them on a graph, and visually see PyTorch grouping similar pieces together.

### 🧠 Version 3: The Mini-Transformer (Attention Engine)

* **What it is:** You take the embeddings from Version 2 and pass them through a couple of **Self-Attention layers** (like a tiny version of GPT).
* **Chess Intelligence & Behavior:** This is where true chess geometry emerges. Version 1 and 2 look at squares in isolation. Version 3's attention mechanism allows the pieces to "talk" to each other across the board. A Bishop on `c1` can actively calculate a relationship with a King on `g8` down an open diagonal. It learns pins, forks, discovered checks, and structural weaknesses.
* **Primary Use:** High-tier move prediction and deep positional evaluation. It allows you to build features like a "similarity search" (e.g., finding historical Grandmaster games that share a similar latent strategic vibe to your current game).
* **Why it's good for Goal B:** It is an incredibly impressive ML project. It transitions from a simple calculator to an engine that maps the abstract "meaning" of a chess position.

## 🎯 Which route should you actually take?

If you want to keep your sanity and ensure success, **build them sequentially.**

Do not try to jump straight to Version 3. Write the code for **Version 1** first to ensure your data loads properly and your model outputs valid moves. Once that works, it takes less than 5 lines of code to swap the input layer for an `nn.Embedding` and turn it into **Version 2**. Only when you are completely comfortable with that behavior should you experiment with adding attention layers for **Version 3**.
